Class format

Each class is either an instructor-led lecture or a student-led discussion about a (set of) research paper(s).

Student-led discussions

In a student-led discussion, a group of 3 students presents a (set of) papers. Each student has an assigned "role":
  • Author: Act as if you are an author of the paper. Describe the paper's motivation, problem definition, method and experimental findings.
  • Reviewer: Act as if you are a reviewer of the paper. Be critical of the work, though not necessarily negative.
  • Empiricist: Implement something related to the paper. This could be redoing an experiment with the authors' code, re-implementing a subset of the paper, or a new analysis (post on Piazza if unsure). Present your findings and code.
We will have a shared pool of slides (hosted on Google presentations, which will be shared a week before. Each student is encouraged to title their slides with "[role]: [student name]" so that the slides are easily identified.

For non-presenting students:

  • Summary and questions: prepare a short summary of one of the class's papers, and at least one discussion question related to the paper. The summary should briefly summarize the motivation, research contributions, method, and at least one key experimental result from the paper (e.g., 1-3 sentences for each of these items).
  • Discussion: non-presenting students are expected to participate in the discussions.

Instructor-led lectures

  • Reading: we will post materials that we recommend reading prior to the course.
  • Attendance: students are expected to attend lectures.

Course project

The course can either be taken without a course project for 6 credits, or with a course project for 12 credits.

The course project simulates a research project on a topic related to the course. This includes (but is not limited to) analysis of a method or paper, replicating a paper's analysis or methods, developing a related method, evaluating a method in a new setting, or other forms of investigation. Project groups are encouraged to propose their own ideas. The instructors will additionally provide a list of ideas that groups can choose from.
  • Project teams: projects are highly recommended to have between 2-4 members. A solo project is possible subject to approval by the instructors.
  • Mid-semester review: project teams will meet with the instructors roughly half way through the semester. Each team will prepare a presentation about the current project status. Instructors will discuss next steps with the team and provide feedback.
  • Final review and writeup: project teams will present their project to the class at the end of the semester. Additionally, each team will prepare a writeup in the style of a workshop paper. The paper describes the project, its current results, challenges faced, and the project's future outlook.
